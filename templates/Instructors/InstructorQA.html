Instructor Q&A

  • question_answerHow Can I Create A Course?
    In OneUp only the administrator can create courses and assign instructors to the courses. If you wish to have a course, please contact the OneUp administrator.
  • question_answerWhat’s A Course Description and How Do I Make One?
    The Course Description is a brief explanation of the content and activities of a course. To create one, in the Course Information menu select Course Description. Simply enter the description that is appropriate for the course and click Save.
  • question_answerWhat are Course Topics?
    Course Topics are the topics that are covered in the course. You can think of them as of chapters in a course textbook. Course topics are used to classify the warm-up challenges, so the students can find easily practice quizzes for the topic they wish to practice. To create a new Topic, in the Course Information menu select Course Topics. Click on Add New Topic and enter a name and, optionally, a position. The topics appear on the Topics list, sorted by the specified positions.
  • question_answerWhat are Activity Categories?
    Activity Categories play a role similar to Course Topics but are specifically for course activities. To create one, in the Course Information menu select Activity Categories. Click on Add New Categories and simply enter a name (i.e. Lab, Homework, etc.). After clicking Submit, the new category will appear in the Activity Categories List.
  • question_answerWhat are Targeted Skills?
    Targeted Skills are exactly skills that are targeted in the specific course. You can use the learning objectives in your course as targeted skills. Skills are linked to problems (at the time of creation of the problems), as students learn skills as they complete problems. To create a Skill, in the Course Information menu select Targeted Skills. Click on Add New Skills and enter a name. After clicking Create, the new skill will be available to be assigned to problems.
  • question_answerWhat are Course Milestones?
    Course Milestones describe all of the required work in the class and how much (course points) each category is worth in total. The total of all milestones shall be equal to the maximum earnable amount of course points in the course (e.g. 100, or 1000, …). The course milestones are used in the creation of the progress bar, which is displayed on the student learning dashboard. To create a new Course Milestone, in the Course Information menu select Course Milestones. Click on Add New Milestone and enter the name, description, and the maximum total of points for your Milestone. After clicking Create, the new Milestone will appear in the list. If you don’t define Course Milestones, you have to disable the Progress Bar, which is shown in the Students/Achievements page. You can do that by going to the Course Information menu and select Course Configuration. Then disable the Progress Bar by turning the switch off.
  • question_answerWhat is the Students Page?
    The Students page contains the information of every student in the course. Students do not create their own accounts, Instructors must make it for them. From this page, Instructors can view their students’ names, email, and the time of their most recent online activity. To add a new student, click on the Add New Student button to be taken to the Add New Student page. Simply fill in the student’s name, email, User Id, and create a password for them. Instructors are responsible for providing students their passwords. Click Submit to add the student to the course. If you wish to add students that are in other courses, then click on the Add Existing Student button to be taken to the Add Students View. Simply select which students you wish to add and click the Add to Class button.
  • question_answerWhat are the Course Configuration options?
    In the Course Information menu, select Course Configuration to view the variety of options available to modify the course. Course Availability makes the course available to students, which is enabled by default. The Set Duration option is used to set the date range for the availability of the course. Once the Course End Date has passed, the course will no longer be available to students, even if Course Availability is enabled. The option to display Serious Challenges by Topics simply groups them by Topics instead of the order in which they were created. The Progress Bar is used to track students progress in the course that works in tandem with Course Milestones. If no Course Milestones are defined, then the Progress Bar has no purpose, thus the option for it to be disabled. Course Gamification is a completely optional feature to instructors that is disabled by default. Once enabled, a Game Features drop-down menu will appear in the site’s main menu which will allow you to configure the desired gamification features.
  • question_answerWhat are Attendance Streaks?
    Attendance streaks allow teachers to set the days of the week their course takes place and track a student’s consecutive attendance. To set an Attendance Streak, go to the Course Information menu and select Attendance Streaks. There will be a calendar that will show the current month. To set an Attendance Streak, select the days of the week you count attendance and click the Submit button under the list of days. The calendar will update with the days you selected and will now have a streak label. Now click the Submit button under the calendar to create the streak.
  • question_answerWhat’s the Difference Between Warm-Up and Serious Challenges?
    Warm-Up challenges are created for students to practice. They are automatically scored and the students can take a warm-up challenge as many times as needed to get a correct answer. Warm-Up challenges will not be shown in the Gradebook, they are primarily for student self-study and feedback. Serious Challenges are typically scheduled graded course exams or quizzes (as included in course syllabus). The primary idea being that the warm-up challenges should prepare the students for the serious challenges.
  • question_answerHow do I Create Challenges?

    In the Course Content menu, select either Warm-Up or Serious Challenges. Click the Add New Challenge button to be taken to the Create Challenge page. After creating a name for the challenge, there are a plethora of configuration options available to Instructors. The Difficulty option has three options: Easy, Medium, and Hard. While selecting a difficulty is optional, it will categorize the challenges created. The option to Randomize Problems will make problems appear in a random order when taken by students instead of the order that they are added to the challenge.

    Instructors can set Availability constraints including visibility, attempts, time limit, and password-lock. By default the challenge will be visible to students, but the date range of that visibility and the due date for the challenge can be set. If a challenge is past its due date but still within the visible date range, it will still be available to students. Also by default, the number of attempts and the time limit are unlimited, but can be restricted. It should be noted that the time limit is in minutes, not seconds.

    Instructors can password lock a challenge. Simply create and confirm a password and distribute it to students as deemed necessary. The Curve is the amount of points added automatically when students take a challenge. The Topics field categorizes the challenge into a specific subject (i.e. Addition, Subtraction, etc). If no topic is assigned to the challenge, it will default to Unspecified, which will automatically place it in the Miscellaneous section. It is highly recommended that Topics are assigned for ease of viewability.

    Instructors also have the option to attach their own Tags to challenges. Attaching Tags allows challenges to be searched for by those Tags. The Feedback Options let Instructors determines how much feedback students get for each challenge. By default, the Display Correct Answers option is enabled, which shows the students the correct answers after they complete the challenge. Both the Correct and Incorrect Answer Feedback are disabled, for the feedback has to be specified for each problem in the challenge. After clicking Save, a new, empty challenge will be created. You then need to create problems for this challenge.
  • question_answerWhat is an Activity?
    An Activity is an assignment in which teachers can upload the required files for the students to complete assignments and students can submit files for the teachers to grade. Teachers can upload as many files as necessary, set how many upload attempts students are allowed, and set what category the Activity belongs to (i.e. Labs, Homework, etc.).
  • question_answerHow do I Create an Activity?

    In the Course Content menu select Activities, then click the Add New Activity button to be taken to the Create Activity page. After setting the Activity name, instructors can add a description that specifies the instructions for the Activity.

    The Default Points are the amount of points possible for the student to obtain. The Display Date range and Dead Line specify how long the activity will be available and when the submission deadline for the activity, respectively. The Activity Category selection option allows assigning the activity a specific category. Activity Categories can be defined by following the steps outlined in What are Activity Categories. If no category is selected, it will default to Uncategorized. The instructor has the option to decide if the Activity is graded and to allow students to upload files. The Activity Graded option simply allows the Activity to be graded by the Instructor.

    Enabling students to upload files means that instructors will be able to view student file submissions and grade them accordingly. Note that if the option to allow student uploads is not checked, they will only be able to submit comments! Instructors can also restrict the amount of Upload Attempts, restricting to a certain amount of submission attempts. Instructors can upload their own files for the student to download to complete the Activity. If they wish to add more than one file, they can simply click the Add Another File to select another file to upload. After clicking Save, the new Activity will appear on the Activities list.

    Problems are very much the sort of problems usually assigned by teachers in the classroom. In OneUp, there are a plethora of problem types: Matching, True/False, Multiple Answer, Parsons Problems, and Dynamic. Problems are designed specifically for Challenges and can be assigned Targeted Skills, Difficulty, and give feedback to students.
  • question_answerHow do I create a Problem?

    In the Course Content menu, select menu. Here you will see the pool of already created, unassigned problems. Click the Create Problem and select which type of problem you wish to create. The required information varies for every problem type, but most of the parameters are universal. The Problem Preview is the name the Instructor sets for the problem that is not seen by the student. The Problem Text contains either the instructions for the problem or the problem itself and will be seen by students. The Difficulty option has three options: Easy, Medium, and Hard. Like with challenges, selecting a difficulty is optional but helps categorize problems. For Parson problems, increasing the difficulty increases the amount of distractors shown to students.

    If gamification is enabled, Skills can be added to problems as can the amount of Skill Points earned for the Skills. Instructors can choose to write feedback for when students answer the problem correctly, incorrectly, or when they complete the challenge with Feedback for Correct Answers, Feedback for Incorrect Answers, and Instructor’s Notes, respectively. The author of the challenge, by default, is the Instructor’s username, but it can be changed if desired.

    It should be noted that problems can only be assigned Points Possible when it belongs to a challenge. If no points are assigned, the problem will give zero points to the student. Tags can be attached to the problem that describe elements of the Problem. Attaching Tags further categorizes the problem and can make searching Problems simpler. After clicking Save, the new problem will be added to the pool, ready to be assigned to a Challenge.

  • question_answerHow do Problems work?
    When a Problem is created, it is not ready for use just yet. The problem must be assigned to a Challenge first, then students will be able to access it. Once a problem is assigned, it is removed from the Problem pool and can be accessed via the Challenge. Problems can only be assigned points once they are added to a challenge. When you delete a problem from a challenge, it is not removed from the database, but returns to the Problem pool ready for reassignment. If you wish to truly Delete a problem, it must be deleted from the Problem pool.

  • question_answerWhat are Dynamic problems?
    Dynamic problems are problems created with Lua code that has far more versatility than static problems. Instructors are capable of creating problems that have randomly generated questions, or using external tools, such as compilers and interpreters, to generate questions. There are two types of dynamic problems: Templates and Raw Lua. A Template problem allows instructors to configure the setup of a problem and then later specify the details of the problem. A Raw Lua problem is more flexible than Template, but most things can be done with Template problems. Instructors should use Raw Lua problems if they want specific results from their code.
  • question_answerHow to Create a Dynamic (Template) Problem?

    Templated dynamic questions allow instructors to write questions and evaluate answers programmatically using Lua code. To create one, go to the Course Content menu and select Problems. Click Create Problem and select Dynamic (Template). The first part of the problem setup has standard configuration options of a Problem (Problem Preview, Skills, etc.). Click the blue Help button with a “?” mark on the top right-hand side of the Problem Info to show the documentation for Template problems.

    The four distinct sections require more nuance: Add Dependency, Dependent Libraries, Set-Up Code, and Template. Instructors can add any Lua Libraries that they have uploaded as dependencies by clicking the Add Dependency drop-down menu and selecting their desired library. The new dependency will appear in the Dependant Libraries section. Lua code is used to set up any needed variables and perform any complex should be included in the Set-Up Code section. The problem itself should be below.

    To display a variable in the Problem Area, it must be enclosed in pipes enclosed in brackets (ex. [|x|]). To include Lua code, it must be enclosed curly braces enclosed in brackets (ex. [{Some code for your thoughts}]). A piece of code that must be included in the problem is the make_answer function which is used to create answer blanks. The make_answer function takes five arguments: The name of the answer blank (displayed to students when describing their results), the type of answer blank, the size, the answer checking function to be used (several of which are provided), and the maximum amount of points the student can receive for the answer blank. A n example make_answer is provided when creating a Template problem in the Template section.

    Instructors can create multi-part problems, where the next part is dependant on the previous one. Simply click the Add Part button to add an additional Template section. Instructors can also test their problems before creating them in by clicking the Try Out Question button at the bottom of the page. The Random Seed field affects any random variables that the Instructor might have included. When the same Random Seed is used, the same problem will be generated. Click Save to add the problem to the problem pool.

  • question_answerHow to Create a Dynamic (Raw Lua) Problem?

    Dynamic problems allow instructors to write problems and evaluate answers programmatically using Lua code. To create one, go to the Course Content menu and select Problems. Click Create Problem and select Dynamic (Raw Lua). The first part of the problem setup has standard configuration options of a Problem (Problem Preview, Skills, etc.). Click the blue Help button with a “?” mark on the top right-hand side of the Problem Info to show the documentation for Raw Lua problems.

    It should be noted that Templated problems and Raw Lua have practically the same capabilities. Instructors should Raw Lua if they want something very specific for their problem to do. Anything done in Raw Lua can generally be done in Templated problems.

    There are four sections that require attention: Add Dependency, Dependent Libraries, Number of Parts, and Code. Both Add Dependency and Dependent Libraries function exactly as they do with Templated problems, allowing Instructors to add any Lua libraries they have uploaded. The Number of Parts is a function similar to the Add Part button in Templated problems. If you wish for a multi-part problem, it must be manually specified by entering the amount of parts in the field. The Code section is where the problem is made using Lua Code. The code, when run, should define the following: part_1_text, evaluate_answer_1, and part_1_max_points.

    The part_1_text function takes no input and produces a string which represents the HTML for the first problem. If you have more than one part to this problem, you should also define similar functions call part_2_text, part_3_text, etc. The evaluate_answer_1 function takes in a table which maps the names of the form inputs from part 1 to values which are the answers provided for those form inputs by the user. It returns a table which maps the names of the form inputs to a table containing two values: “success”, which maps to a boolean indicating whether or not the answer was sufficient to continue to the next part and "value", which maps to a floating point number indicating how many points were awarded for that answer. The part_1_max_points function takes no input, but returns a table which maps the name of each input to the maximum possible number of points a student could earn for that problem. An example of proper Raw Lua code is in the Code section when creating the problem.

    As with Template problems, Instructors can test their problems before creating them by pressing the Try Out Question button at the bottom of the page. The Random Seed field affects any random variables that the Instructor might have included. Click Save to add the problem to the problem pool.

  • question_answerWhat are Rule Conditions?
    Rule Conditions are the backbone of the gamification system of OneUp. In broad terms, they are the rules that are checked to see if specific conditions are met. If they are, then an event specified by the Instructor occurs. These events can vary: A badge is awarded, Virtual Currency is awarded, a new Challenge is unlocked, or some other situation. While sounding complex and intimidating, creating Rule Conditions can be thought of simply thought of as rules specified as a boolean expression. An event only fires if the proper conditions are met.
  • question_answerHow do I Create a Rule?

    If you’re creating a badge, setting VC earning rules, or configuring Progressive Unlocking, you’ll need to specify the Rule Conditions. The Rule Conditions section is always found at the bottom of the page. Rule Conditions work by having a variable of some sort on the left-hand side (i.e Activities Completed, Badges Earned, etc) and having the required condition on the right-hand side. A full Rule Condition would read like this: Percent of student score for Challenges X,Y,Z is more than or equal to 75.

    The Rule Conditions section is a drag-and-drop interface: drag a condition type into the dotted rectangle to begin modifying it. Note the small AND with another dotted rectangle under it once a rule type is dragged in. This is for when you want multiple conditions to be true in order for the rule to fire (i.e. x = 2 AND y = 3). By default, an Atom condition type is the first condition. There are three condition types: Atom, OR, and Forall.

    The Atom condition is a single conditional statement (i.e. if (x = 5) would only be true if x = 5). Atom is the primary condition type, for they can apply to most situations. By default, an Atom condition type is the first condition in the Rule Conditions section. The Atom condition has three parameters: the variable, the operator, and the threshold. The variable is the specific condition that is being checked (i.e. student percentage score on a challenge). The operator parameter has five options: greater than (>), less than (<), equal to (=), greater than or equal to (>=), and less than or equal to (<=). The operators functions just as they do mathematically. The Threshold option simply specifies the value required for the condition to trigger. By default, the Threshold is a number value, but if the Value checkbox is unchecked the Instructor can choose another variable to compare to the first.

    The OR condition specifies that one of two or more conditions can be true and the entire condition would be true (i.e. the statement if (x < 2) OR (x > 4) would be true if x = 1 or 5). OR is useful if the Instructor desires multiple ways to satisfy a condition.

    The Forall condition allows Instructors to specify how broad the rule is. There are three selectors in Forall: For, of the following, and items to add. The For selector simply specifies whether each or all of the specified assignments will be considered. The Of the following selector allows the Instructor to select from challenges, activities, topics, and categories. The Items to add selector allows the Instructor to add one or more of the items specified in the Of the following selector. The checkbox next to the Items to add selector will select all of the items available if checked (i.e All Activities). At the bottom of the Forall condition is another dotted rectangle. The Atom or OR condition dragged there will work just the same, but with the added restrictions set by the Instructor. Once an Atom condition is placed, the available variables become more specific depending on the item type selected in the Of the following selector. To remove a condition from the area, drag it to the Trash box at the upper left of the section.
  • question_answerWhat are Badges?
    Badges are a form of an award a student can recieve for completing tasks specified by the Instructor. There are three types of Badges: Automatic, Manual, and Time-Period based. Automatic Badges are awarded according to Rule Conditions defined by the Instructor. Manual Badges are badges with no conditions that are assigned to individual students. Time-Period Based Badges are awarded at specific time intervals to a limited number of students who meet certain conditions (i.e. students with a 5-day attendance streak). The main purpose of Badges is to give students a sense of achievement and progress as they advance through the course. Badges that students earn are shown in their Achievements page and the Latest Badges section of the Course Home page.

  • question_answerWhat is Progressive Unlocking?
    Progressive Unlocking is a gamification feature that gives the Instructors the ability to lock certain content from students until specific conditions are met. There are a plethora of possible conditions, including if a certain score is reached on a challenged or an amount of badges are earned. The primary purpose of this feature is to have students complete challenges/activities in a specific order instead of in any order without having to create assignments one at a time.

  • question_answerWhat is Virtual Currency?
    Virtual Currency is the currency awarded to students for completing tasks and meeting specific conditions set by the Instructor. The main purpose of Virtual Currency is to provide a great incentive for students to complete coursework and routinely track their progress. Virtual Currency can be spent on useful rewards at the discretion of the Instructor: Deadline Extensions, Assignment Resubmissions, Extra Credit, Lowest Grade replacement, and a great host of other benefits. As with Badges, there are three different types of Virtual Currency earning rules: Manual, Automatic, and Time-Period Based. Manual earning rules are rules with no conditions that are assigned to individual students. Time-Period Based rules are awarded at specific time intervals to a limited number of students who meet certain conditions (i.e. students with a 5-day attendance streak). The main purpose of Badges is to give students a sense of achievement and progress as they advance through the course.

  • question_answerHow do I Create a Time-Period Based Virtual Currency Earning Rule?
    In the Game Features menu, hover over Virtual Currency and then select Time-Period Earning Rules. Here you can view/modify all Time-Period rules and the amount of currency they award. Click the Create New Periodic Earning Rule button to be taken to the Time-Period Rule Creation page. The Rule Name and Rule Description fields give the rule a name and provide a description for the conditions for earning the currency, respectively. The primary difference here is the Time-Period conditions. These are not unlike Rule Conditions, but much more streamlined to simplify the process. There are four condition options that require attention: Give To, Number of Students, Time Period, and the Condition section. The Give To option allows Instructors to give the VC to either a top number of students (Top #), all students, or randomly. The Number of Students option sets the amount of students of the class will earn the VC and only appears if the Top # option is selected. The Time Period drop-down menu specifies how often the VC will be awarded to students (i.e. Daily, Weekly, etc.). The Condition section functions much like a Rule Condition, but more specific options. The Operator and Threshold option functions the same as with Rule Conditions. Additionally, the Course Bucks Earned with this Rule field is simply the amount of Course Bucks that will be earned by the students once the Time-Period completes a cycle. Once all parameters are satisfactory, click Create Rule to add the new Time-Period Rule to the Periodic Virtual Currency Rules page.

  • question_answerWhat is Dueling?
    Dueling is a gamification feature that allows students to challenge each other to obtain higher scores on assignments for Virtual Currency. Dueling is entirely up to the students, with very little Instructor involvement in the process. The primary goal of Dueling is to provide students with an incentive and increase their competitiveness in a course.
  • question_answerHow do I Enable Duels?
    Duels are a gamification feature that is disabled by default. If you wish to enable Duels, ensure that gamification is enabled. Then, in the Game Features menu select Gamification Configuration. Locate the Challenging Classmates section and then find the option to enable the feature.
  • question_answerAre Students Obligated to Accept Challenges?
    No. If a student declines or doesn’t respond to another student’s challenge, the duel is void and no Virtual Currency is awarded.
  • question_answerWhat Challenges can Students Duel Over?
    Students can only duel over Warm-Up Challenges they have not completed. Also, students cannot select specific challenges; instead, challenges are selected at random by challenge Topic (ex. Addition, Multiplication). If either student has completed a challenge, it is automatically ineligible to be available for the duel.
  • question_answerWhat Role does the Instructor Play in Duels?
    The only roles the Instructor has is creating the Warm-Up Challenges students take and setting how much VC is awarded to students for participation and victory. To set the amounts, go to the Game Features menu and select Gamification Configuration. Navigate to the Classmates Challenges option. Click Enable the use of duels and call-outs and click Save to enable Duels. The option for challenging students will now appear on the students’ side.
